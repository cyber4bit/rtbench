ABSTRACT: Retention time (RT) can provide orthogonal
information to mass spectra, supporting the qualitative identi-
fication. However, RT is influenced by experimental conditions and
column parameters, and it is difficult to have a large amount of RT
data in the user’s experimental conditions. Hence, various machine
learning methods, including advanced deep learning approaches,
have been developed for RT prediction. However, most of them
were limited to a given column and operational conditions. In the
meantime, data sparsity often hinders the prediction performance.
In this study, we propose an MDL-TL method that combines
multiple data sets to jointly train the base model. MDL-TL
vectorizes the column and conditions (chromatographic parame-
ters, CPs) using word2vec and autoencoders, and distinguishes the
data sets from different chromatographic experiments by including the CPs in the compound representation. This not only augments
the data but also introduces the CPs into the RT prediction, allowing the pretrained model to be efficiently transferred to different
target systems by fine-tuning. MDL-TL was evaluated against five popular deep learning approaches and four machine learning
approaches on 14 reversed-phase liquid chromatography data sets and 14 hydrophilic interaction liquid chromatography data sets,
respectively. The results show that our method surpassed the compared methods, including transfer learning methods based on the
METLIN small molecule retention time (SMRT) data set, in mean absolute error, median absolute error, mean relative error, and R
2
in most cases, demonstrating that MDL-TL is a promising approach for predicting RTs for various chromatographic systems and
operational conditions.
Metabolomics studies the systematic changes of metabolites in
organisms under the interference of internal and external
factors
1
and has shown its power in many fields, such as human
health study,
2−6
environmental pollution,
7
and nutrition
research.
8
Liquid chromatography−mass spectrometry (LC−
MS) is one of the main analytical techniques for the
metabolomics study. Metabolite identification is a bottleneck
in nontargeted metabolomics,
9−11
with only about 18% of
detected compounds being annotatable .
12
At present, metabolite identification is typically performed
by matching mass spectra to entries in spectral databases.
13
The identification accuracy needs to be greatly improved,
partly due to a high false-positive rate. Chromatography
retention time (RT) can provide information orthogonal to
mass spectra, help reduce the false positives, and improve the
accuracy of compound identification.
14
However, the number
of compounds with known RT under specific chromatographic
conditions on a given column system is limited, primarily due
to the limited availability of standard samples. Therefore,
accurately predicting the RTs of compounds in a given
chromatographic experiment is highly meaningful.
There are two main types of RT prediction approaches,
15
physicochemical and statistical retention models. Quantitative
structure-retention relationships (QSRR),
16
a widely used
statistical retention model, is one of the most common
approaches. It can directly predict RTs of compounds through
regression without requiring chemical experiments. Machine
learning (ML) methods, such as random forest (RF), gradient
boosting (GB), support vector regression, and neural networks,
are all QSRR methods and have been applied to RT
prediction.
17−23
Bouwmeester et al.
24
compared seven ML
algorithms for RT prediction and discovered that different
methods show varying performance across different data sets.
In recent years, deep learning (DL) has achieved a state-of-
the-art performance in RT prediction. Due to the high cost of
standard samples and the labor-intensive process of exper-
imentally determined RTs, the amount of labeled data (i.e.,
compounds with known RTs) in a given column system and at

data sparsity limits the performance of ML methods, including
DL approaches, in RT prediction.
To mitigate the issue of data sparsity, transfer learning (TL)
offers a promising solution. For a new task, TL fine-tunes the
pretrained model to improve model performance. Since the
pretrained model has learned the prior knowledge on the
source task, the fine-tuned model can perform effectively in
sparse data situations. However, pretraining should be carried
out on large-scale data sets to learn high-quality prior
knowledge, but the data sets for RT prediction are usually
limited in size, except for the METLIN small molecule
retention time (SMRT) data set, which contains 80,038
compounds.
25
Most of the existing TL studies for RT
prediction
26−29
worked on the SMRT for pretraining.
DNNpwa-TL
26
utilizes the weighted autoencoder to pretrain
the deep neural network on SMRT, GNN-RT
27
pretrains the
graph neural networks (GNN) on SMRT, 1D CNN
28
pretrains the convolutional neural network (CNN), and
MDC-ANN
29
pretrains the model on SMRT and determines
the optimal compound representation combination in fine-
tuning.
SMRT is a reversed-phase liquid chromatography (RPLC)
data set. The base model pretrained on it is not suitable for
transferring to other chromatographic modes, for example,
hydrophilic interaction liquid chromatography (HILIC).
Additionally, most RT prediction approaches focus exclusively
on molecular representations, overlooking the chromato-
graphic parameters (CPs), thereby limiting the transferability
of prediction models across different chromatographic settings.
MultiConditionRT
30
attempts to address this limitation by
incorporating CPs, such as column type and eluent
composition, into the model by numerically encoding them
as input features.
Though the labeled data for RT prediction in a single
experiment are usually scarce, there exist many data sets from
different experiments. Hence, if multiple small data sets can be
combined to pretrain and learn prior knowledge, the
performance of RT prediction can be significantly improved.
RTs are determined not only by the compound structure but
also by the CPs, commonly referred to as chromatographic
columns and operational conditions (CCOCs), leading to RT
variation for the same compound across different chromato-
graphic systems. A key challenge in integrating multiple small
data sets for pretraining lies in effectively encoding CCOC
information into the compound representation to distinguish
the data from different experiments.
In this study, we propose a new RT prediction method that
combines Multiple Data sets to Learn the prior knowledge and
establishes the RT prediction model for a specific chromato-
graphic experiment by Transfer Learning (MDL-TL). To mark
the data sets from different experiments and incorporate CPs
into the learning model, MDL-TL encodes the chromato-
graphic column information and operational conditions using
autoencoders (AEs) and Word2Vec. Then, MDL-TL pretrains
on the union of multiple data sets and transfers the learned
knowledge to the target task.
To evaluate the performance of MDL-TL, we investigated
two different training strategies: pretraining using the data sets
from the same chromatographic system and from different
chromatographic systems. We also compared MDL-TL with
the popular ML and DL methods. The results show that (1)
combining multiple small data sets for pretraining could
improve RT prediction performance, and MDL-TL out-
performed the compared methods, including the SMRT-
based transfer learning methods, in most cases; (2) pretraining
with data sets that have CPs similar to those of the target task
yields better performance. The source code for the model is
available at https://github.com/DLUT-datas/MDL-TL.git.

METHODS
The RT of a given compound is determined by its molecular
structure, operational conditions, and chromatographic column
parameters. Operational conditions mainly include mobile
phase composition, gradient elution program, flow rate, and
column temperature. Column parameters comprise the
chromatographic column type, length, inner diameter, particle
size, and dead time.
To address data sparsity and enhance RT prediction
performance, MDL-TL integrates multiple data sets to pretrain
together. Since the RTs of the same compound vary across
different chromatographic methods (CMs), MDL-TL vector-
izes CPs to distinguish the data sets from different CMs and
incorporates the representation of chromatographic column
information and operational conditions into the prediction
model. As shown in Figure 1, MDL-TL consists of four main
components: (1) encoding CPs; (2) representing compounds
by fusing molecular representation with CP settings; (3)
pretraining the base model on the union of multiple data sets;
and (4) fine-tuning the base model to construct the final RT
prediction model.

A CP encoder (CPEncoder) (Figure 1A−D) is designed to
encode CPs, including chromatographic column information
and operational conditions. CPEncoder consists of two AEs
(AE-1 and AE-2), a Word2Vec module, and a concatenation
module. AE-1 encodes the gradient elution program, AE-2
encodes the mobile phase composition, and the Word2Vec
module encodes the column type. The concatenation module
integrates the representations of the gradient elution program,
mobile phase composition, and column information to
generate the CP vector.
The polarity ranges and physicochemical properties of
different compounds can exhibit substantial variability. To
ensure effective separation of target compounds, the gradient
program must be adjusted based on the compound types and
the analytical requirements. For different analytical scenarios,
some compounds may need multisegment gradients (see
examples in Table S1), while others can be separated in a
linear gradient program. Therefore, in addition to differences
in mobile phase composition, gradient programs also vary
across experiments. Using a vector to represent the gradient
program, such as the vector concatenating the segments, is
straightforward. However, this may result in variable-length
vectors across different CPs. Hence, an AE (AE-1) is
employed.

Pretraining by Integrating Multiple Data Sets. Most
RT data sets are small, and a small sample size tends to affect
the prediction performance, making the model overfitting.
MDL-TL combines multiple data sets to address this issue and
enhance the prediction model.
Suppose n > 0 data sets are collected. For data set j (1 ≤ j ≤
n), MRFM computes the comprehensive representation
X

X

X-Val is for evaluating the models and selecting the best one.
Deep neural network (DNN) is one of the most popular
models in DL due to its strong feature extraction capabilities.
MDL-TL employs DNN as the base model, using mean
absolute error (MAE) as the loss function (detailed
descriptions can be found in Supporting Information-1).

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In this study, hyperparameter optimization was conducted via
automated grid searching and embedded in the method (see
Supporting Information-1).
Performance Examination. MDL-TL was evaluated
under both RPLC and HILIC chromatographic modes. First,
MDL-TL was compared with five DL methods and four ML
methods on the RPLC data sets (Table 1). Figure 2 shows the
comparison results with DL methods in MAE and R
2
, and
Figure S2 shows the results in MedAE and MRE. MDL-TL
outperformed the five DL methods in most cases (see detailed
discussion in Supporting Information-1). MDL-TL did not
perform well on certain data sets (although it still out-
performed the baseline methods). We analyzed the similarity
of CP vectors among the data sets (Figure S3) and found that
the data sets with lower performance tended to have lower
similarity to the rest. Therefore, we recommend using data sets
with chromatographic conditions similar to the target data set
for pretraining to achieve better performance.
Figures 3 and S4 report the comparison of MDL-TL against
the four ML methods and show that MDL-TL achieved the
best performance in MAE, MedAE, R
2
, and MRE on all data
sets.
The above results illustrate that MDL-TL can produce an
RT prediction model with higher performance than the
popular ML methods, including DL methods. Now, most of
the existing transfer learning methods for RT prediction
pretrain on the large RPLC data set, SMRT containing 80,038
compounds. The experiments show that MDL-TL, pretraining
on multiple data sets and incorporating CPs into the model,
performed better than the TL methods based on SMRT in
most cases. The total compound number of the 14 data sets is
much smaller than that of SMRT. This study provides a
feasible and efficient new method for RT prediction.
Second, MDL-TL was evaluated on the HILIC data sets
(Table 1) against three DL methods, 1D CNN, GCN, and
RGCN, as well as four ML methods, RF, GB, LASSO, and
XGBoost (GNN-RT and GIN, pretrained on SMRT, were not
included here). Tables S5−S8 report the result and show that
MDL-TL demonstrates superior performance compared to the
baseline approaches.
Transfer learning, which refers to a pretrained model being
reused in another target task, is a popular method for
addressing data sparsity. The pretraining quality affects the
performance for the target task. Usually, the experiment is
conducted on a source task with rich data. While for RT
prediction, the number of labeled data is often very small
except for SMRT. Hence, in RT prediction, there are not many
studies of TL on HILIC data. The experiment shows that our
method is an efficient method to address the data sparsity and

Performance on the 14 External RPLC Data Sets.
Once a base model is established, can it be directly used for a
new task after fine-tuning? To investigate this, MDL-TL was
evaluated on 14 external RPLC data sets (Table S4) that were
not involved in the pretraining. The base model pretrained on
the RPLC data sets in Table 1 was directly fine-tuned on the
external data sets. Tables 2, 3, S9, and S10 are the comparison
of MDL-TL with DL and ML methods. MDL-TL surpassed
the compared baselines in most cases. A detailed discussion is
given in Supporting Information-1.
Hence, the pretrained base model can be effectively
transferred to the target tasks, even if the data of the target
task were not involved in pretraining. For a new task, the
existing pretrained base model can be directly adopted, or a
new one can be pretrained by combining the data of the new
task with the previously collected data. Usually, the latter
method is more efficient than the former one.
Performance Examination on 14 Mixed Data Sets. To
investigate whether the data used for pretraining must be from
the same chromatographic mode, MDL-TL was evaluated on
the 14 mixed data sets (Table S3). The results (Tables S11−
S14) indicate that MDL-TL pretraining on the mixed data sets
outperformed the compared methods but showed a worse fit
than MDL-TL pretraining on the data sets from the same
chromatographic mode. Therefore, it is recommended to
pretrain on data sets from the same chromatographic system as
the target task for better performance.

CONCLUSIONS
Compound identification remains a major bottleneck in
metabolomics research. RT can provide orthogonal informa-
tion to mass spectra and improve identification accuracy.
Developing methods capable of reliably predicting RTs is of
considerable importance.
This study proposes a novel method, MDL-TL, for
constructing high-performance RT prediction models. MDL-
TL employs AEs and Word2Vec to vectorize CPs and
combines multiple data sets for joint pretraining. The CP
vectorization enables the model to distinguish data from
different chromatographic data sets and facilitates the
incorporation of chromatographic information into the RT
prediction model. Extensive experiments on public data sets
demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
The main novelties lie in (1) MDL-TL combines multiple
data sets to jointly pretrain, effectively addressing data sparsity
in RT prediction across various chromatographic systems and
providing a feasible and efficient solution for RT prediction.
(2) MDL-TL vectorizes the CPs and integrates them into the
RT prediction model, enhancing its predictive performance.
For a given chromatography experiment, multiple data sets
with similar chromatographic conditions can be collected to
jointly pretrain and acquire transferable knowledge. This
knowledge can then be fine-tuned for the target system to
perform the RT prediction. Alternatively, a pretrained base
model can be directly fine-tuned, making the proposed
approach both efficient and practical in real-world applications.
In pretraining, different chromatographic systems are distin-
guished by their CP settings. Incorporating CPs into the RT
prediction model not only improves predictive performance
but also facilitates the investigation of chromatographic
retention mechanisms.
